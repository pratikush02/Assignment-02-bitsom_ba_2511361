Question: "A healthcare startup is building a patient management system. One engineer recommends MySQL; another recommends MongoDB. Given your understanding of ACID vs BASE and the CAP theorem, which would you recommend and why? Would your answer change if they also needed to add a fraud detection module?"

Answer:
When choosing between MySQL and MongoDB for a healthcare startup’s patient management system, the decision hinges on data consistency requirements and the CAP theorem trade-offs. Healthcare data is highly sensitive, often requiring strict ACID compliance (Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability). MySQL, as a relational database, excels here: it enforces schemas, supports transactions, and ensures strong consistency. This is critical for patient records, prescriptions, and billing, where even minor inconsistencies could have serious consequences. Under the CAP theorem, MySQL prioritizes Consistency and Availability over Partition Tolerance, which aligns well with healthcare’s need for reliable, accurate data.

MongoDB, on the other hand, is a BASE-oriented (Basically Available, Soft state, Eventually consistent) NoSQL database. It offers flexibility with unstructured data, horizontal scalability, and faster development cycles. This makes it attractive for handling diverse data types like medical images, wearable device logs, or patient notes. However, its eventual consistency model may pose risks if strict transactional integrity is required.

If the system’s primary focus is patient management, I would recommend MySQL because of its robust ACID guarantees and schema enforcement, ensuring compliance with healthcare regulations like HIPAA.  

However, if the startup also needs to add a fraud detection module, the answer shifts. Fraud detection often involves analyzing large volumes of heterogeneous data (claims, logs, behavioral patterns) in near real-time. MongoDB’s scalability and flexible schema make it better suited for this analytical workload. In such a case, a polyglot architecture could be ideal: use MySQL for core patient records where consistency is paramount, and MongoDB for fraud detection and analytics where scalability and flexibility matter most. This hybrid approach balances reliability with agility, ensuring both patient safety and advanced fraud prevention.
